④ Management Commentary

Earnings Call Tone — FY2025 (Feb 12, 2026) | CEO: Fernando Fernandez

CEO Fernando Fernandez, who replaced Hein Schumacher in mid-2025, set an explicitly optimistic but sober tone: "In 2025 we became a simpler, sharper, and faster Unilever." He emphasized that the Ice Cream demerger (completed December 6, 2025) fundamentally repositions the company toward higher-growth categories. Key strategic pillars articulated: (1) Building a brand portfolio centred on Beauty, Wellbeing, and Personal Care; (2) Prioritising premium segments and digital commerce; (3) Anchoring growth in the US and India as core developed/EM anchors.

CFO commentary confirmed the productivity programme delivered €670M of cumulative savings by year-end, ahead of the €650M target. On the Q4 2025 earnings call, Fernandez stated: "Our key metrics don't change — volume growth, positive mix, and gross margin expansion." Management acknowledged slowing markets entering 2026, particularly in emerging markets (Brazil, India, Indonesia, China), but maintained guidance for 4%–6% underlying sales growth for FY2026, targeting the bottom end of that range.

Guidance FY2026: Underlying sales growth 4–6% (bottom end); at least 2% volume growth; modest underlying operating margin improvement above FY2025's 20.0%; further €130M productivity savings to be delivered in 2026; new €1.5B share buyback for H1 2026 announced.

⑤ Red Flags

Red Flag 1 — Margins Compressing

Status: WATCH — Partially Resolved, Structural Tension Remains

GAAP operating margin collapsed from 18.4% in FY2021 to 16.2% in FY2022 as input cost inflation (particularly edible oils, petrochemicals, packaging) far outpaced pricing. Recovery followed: 17.7% in FY2023, but then a further GAAP dip to 15.5% in FY2024 due to accelerated restructuring charges (€850M) and disposal losses. FY2025 (continuing ops) recovered to 17.9% GAAP, with underlying operating margin reaching 20.0%. The GAAP vs. Non-GAAP gap of approximately 260bps in FY2025 is material and warrants scrutiny — restructuring is classified as non-underlying but has been a recurring annual cost since 2022. Gross margin trajectory is genuinely positive (45.0% in FY2024, highest in a decade per IR). However, accelerating brand and marketing investment (up 120bps to 15.5% of turnover in FY2024) partially offsets gross margin gains — a deliberate trade-off but one that constrains net margin expansion.

Red Flag 2 — Volume Declining

Status: RESOLVED in 2024 — Now Watch for 2026 Deceleration

Volume was a persistent problem for Unilever from FY2022 (−1.1%) through most of FY2023 (−0.2%). The pricing-led growth strategy of 2022–2023 — necessary to protect margins during inflation — came at a volume cost. FY2024 marked a genuine recovery: four consecutive quarters of underlying volume growth above 2.0%, with full-year volumes up +2.9%. FY2025 volume growth decelerated to +1.5%, which management attributed to slowing market conditions rather than competitive share loss. Key risk: Indonesia and China markets saw volume resets in 2025. Power Brands delivered +2.2% volume in FY2025 vs. non-Power Brands declining — a widening bifurcation. FY2026 guidance of "at least 2% volume growth" is achievable but requires improving EM momentum.

Red Flag 3 — FCF Volatile

Status: CONFIRMED — 2022 Breach >25%; 2025 Decline Flagged

Company-reported FCF fell from approximately €6.4B in FY2021 to approximately €4.3B in FY2022 — a decline well exceeding the 25% trigger threshold. This breach was driven by a perfect storm: negative working capital swing, gross margin collapse, and rising capex. FCF recovered to €7.1B in FY2023 and €6.9B in FY2024, then fell again to €5.9B in FY2025 — a −14.5% decline, primarily from Ice Cream demerger-related costs (management explicitly cited "down €0.4bn primarily due to Ice Cream demerger costs"). Macrotrends data (USD-translated) shows a similar pattern. The 100% cash conversion in FY2025 is a positive quality indicator, but FCF is now structurally lower as the base revenue base (post-demerger) has shrunk. FY2026 FCF trajectory will be a critical watch item.

Red Flag 4 — Capex >70% of Operating Cash Flow

Status: NOT PRESENT — Capex is well-controlled

Unilever's capital expenditure has been disciplined. In FY2024, operating cash flow was approximately €10.0B and capex approximately €1.7B — a capex/OpCF ratio of approximately 17%. TTM data from stockanalysis.com shows operating cash flow of $9.8B and capex of −$1.66B, a ratio of approximately 17%. This is far below the 70% red-flag threshold and consistent with Unilever's asset-light model (manufacturing outsourced in part, brand-heavy business). Capital expenditure guidance going forward includes targeted investments in premium supply chain capabilities, but no indication of a step-change in capex intensity. This is a sector-standard, healthy level.

Red Flag 5 — Debt Growing in Downturn

Status: WATCH — Net Debt Elevated but Improving Post-Demerger

Net debt peaked at approximately €24.5B in FY2024 and declined to approximately €23.1B in FY2025, with a net debt-to-EBITDA ratio of approximately 2.0x — manageable for an investment-grade FMCG company. However, Net Debt/Equity remains elevated at approximately 161% (TTM, stockanalysis.com: D/E 1.61x). During the 2022 inflationary downturn, net debt increased even as earnings declined — a classic red flag pattern. Unilever has been actively refinancing at higher rates; management noted net finance costs of approximately 3% on average net debt expected for FY2025. A further €1.5B bond issuance in H2 2025 adds to gross debt. The demerger improved the ratio but did not fundamentally resolve the structural leverage. For an FMCG company with stable cash flows, this level of leverage is acceptable — but leaves limited buffer for another inflationary shock.

Red Flag 6 — Management Using "Uncertainty / Headwinds" Language

Status: CONFIRMED — Pervasive in FY2025 and FY2026 Guidance

Management language in the FY2025 results and subsequent analyst calls is replete with hedging: "despite slowing markets," "currency headwinds," "macroeconomic pressures in Latin America," "challenges in Indonesia and China," "slower market conditions." The FY2026 guidance was explicitly set "at the bottom end" of the 4%–6% USG range — a downward signal within already-cautious guidance. The earnings call transcript shows analysts from Barclays, UBS, and HSBC probing on emerging market outlook, US volume, and Brazil distribution. CEO Fernandez also stated the company is navigating a "US-Iran war" related hiring pause (CNBC, March 31, 2026) — an exogenous macro factor. While some uncertainty language is warranted given genuine macro conditions, the cumulative weight of hedging terms across guidance and earnings discourse is a watch item under Lynch's framework for management honesty vs. excuse-making.

⑥ Invert — Charlie Munger Style

Why Unilever Could Be a Bad Long-Term Investment

Unilever is structurally exposed to the very markets it most relies on. Emerging markets represent approximately 58% of sales — but these markets are also the most volatile, the most currency-challenged, and increasingly competitive with local champions (especially in India, Indonesia, and China). The company has spent four years restructuring, demerging, and reorganising, yet GAAP operating margins in FY2025 (17.9%) remain below FY2021 (18.4%) despite all the savings programs. If the productivity programme merely offsets structural cost inflation without delivering genuine margin expansion above the pre-crisis baseline, the entire 2022–2026 transformation delivers no net shareholder value. Meanwhile, the dividend has been effectively frozen for three years (FY2021–FY2023), and earnings per share grew a cumulative ~8% over five years — a CAGR of just ~1.6%. At 15x forward earnings, the stock may look cheap relative to peers, but not relative to its actual growth rate. The PEG of 11x is egregious by Lynch standards.

What Invalidates the Investment Despite Good-Looking Financials

The apparent improvement in underlying operating margin (18.4% in FY2024, 20.0% in FY2025 on a continuing basis) is partly a composition effect — the Ice Cream demerger removed a lower-margin, capital-intensive, weather-sensitive business from the portfolio. Reported improvement in ROIC (from ~14.8% in FY2022 to 18.1% in FY2024) similarly benefits from the removal of low-ROIC assets. If the remaining business (Beauty, Personal Care, Home Care, Foods) was already the higher-quality core, the "improvement" was always latent and does not represent new value creation by management. Additionally, the GAAP vs. Non-GAAP gap is a recurring structural feature — Unilever has booked restructuring charges in every year from FY2020 through FY2025. This means "underlying" earnings consistently overstate economic earnings by 200–300bps of margin.

Assumptions That Must Go Right for the Bull Case

The consensus Buy case (~$65 target) requires: (1) Emerging market volumes recover to 3%+ growth in 2026–2027, particularly India and Indonesia; (2) Premium segment (Beauty & Wellbeing) sustains double-digit growth globally; (3) Productivity savings flow through to GAAP margins — not just underlying; (4) Currency headwinds (5.9% adverse impact in FY2025) moderate or reverse; (5) The McCormick-Unilever food business transaction (Unilever sold food brands to McCormick for ~$16B, announced March 2026) is executed cleanly without integration risk or disruption; (6) No further major commodity inflation cycle in 2026–2027 (particularly palm oil, edible oils). If any two of these factors disappoint simultaneously, the investment thesis unravels rapidly given the current leverage level and limited FCF buffer.

Structural Weaknesses That Persist Regardless of Management Quality

First, Unilever operates in mature, low-growth categories globally — personal wash, laundry, condiments. Volume growth above 3% sustainably requires either market share gains or category expansion, both of which are structurally difficult in a world of private-label competition and GLP-1 drug adoption affecting food volumes. Second, the company's geographic footprint — 58% EM — means it will always be subject to outsized currency translation risk. Third, unlike P&G (ROIC 22%, D/E 69%) or L'Oréal (ROIC 14%, D/E 28%), Unilever's balance sheet carries significant structural leverage that limits strategic optionality during downturns. Fourth, the Ben & Jerry's political governance overhang — while a minor financial item — has demonstrated that Unilever's complex stakeholder management model can produce headline reputational risk disproportionate to the financial impact, and the relationship between the brand and parent company remains legally and reputationally complex.

⑦ Golden Tips — Analyst Watching Points

Golden Tip 1 — GAAP vs. Non-GAAP: Watch the Restructuring Addiction

Unilever's "underlying operating margin" (UOM) is their flagship metric — and it consistently runs 200–300bps above the GAAP operating margin. In FY2025, UOM was 20.0% vs. GAAP operating margin of approximately 17.9% — a gap of approximately 210bps. Management justifies this gap via restructuring charges, disposal losses, and one-off items. The problem: Unilever has booked "non-underlying" items in every single year since at least FY2020. What started as a crisis-management tool (2022 restructuring) has become a structural reporting feature. For any Lynch-style investor, you must anchor valuation to GAAP earnings and GAAP FCF. The productivity programme claims €800M of savings (€670M delivered by end-2025) — but annual restructuring costs of approximately €850M in FY2024 effectively consumed all of those savings in the same year. Watch whether FY2026 marks a genuine decline in non-underlying charges.

Golden Tip 2 — FCF vs. Net Income: The 2022 Canary

In FY2022, Unilever's net income was approximately $7.6B but company-reported FCF fell to approximately €4.3B — a dramatic gap. The divergence was driven by working capital deterioration (inventory build as costs surged, payables tightened) and capex increases. This is one of the clearest signals available to an analyst that reported profits are not translating to cash. Always cross-check Unilever's net income against FCF, and compare both against operating cash flow. In FY2024 and FY2025, cash conversion returned to approximately 100–106%, restoring quality. But vigilance is required: if commodity costs spike in 2026–2027 (as management has cautioned), the FY2022 playbook could repeat. FCF yield (currently ~4.9%) is the most honest valuation anchor for this company — not the P/E multiple based on non-GAAP EPS.

Golden Tip 3 — Revenue Up, Margins Down: The 2022–2024 Warning Pattern

From FY2021 to FY2022, Unilever's revenue grew +14.7% — the best revenue growth in over a decade. Simultaneously, gross margin collapsed nearly 250bps and GAAP operating margin fell approximately 220bps. This is a textbook example of revenue growth being driven by price (not volume), with costs rising faster than prices. Lynch's framework specifically warns against mistaking price-driven revenue growth for genuine business strength. When a company's revenue jumps +14% but earnings per share barely move — as happened in FY2022 — the growth is illusory. Investors who anchored to the revenue headline got burned. Always dissect: how much of revenue growth is volume vs. price? Unilever now reports these transparently (UVG and UPG) — use them. In FY2025, USG of 3.5% was composed of 1.5% volume and 2.0% price — a healthier mix than 2022's 11%+ price / −1%+ volume split.

Golden Tip 4 — Capex Pattern: Maintenance vs. Growth Investment

Unilever's capex-to-revenue ratio has been low and stable — approximately 2.5–2.8% of revenue historically, consistent with a brand-heavy business where competitive advantage is built through marketing, formulation science, and distribution rather than physical plant. This low capital intensity is a genuine quality characteristic and helps explain the relatively high FCF conversion (operating cash flow less capex = FCF). However, two watch items: (1) Unilever is now investing in premium supply chain capabilities (>10 agile production lines for innovations; €150M European supply chain investment), which could modestly increase capex intensity in 2026–2027. (2) Post-demerger, capex requirements for the Ice Cream cold-chain are removed — making the remaining business structurally more capital-light. Watch for any capex guidance changes in Q1 2026 trading statement (expected April 30, 2026). If capex/OpCF climbs above 30%, it would be a genuine shift in the investment profile.
