Note on the GLP-1 Franchise: Growth Engine Has Stalled

Novo Nordisk's FY21–FY24 growth was driven almost entirely by semaglutide: Ozempic (diabetes) and Wegovy (obesity). Revenue compounded at ~20% CAGR over 4 years driven by volume expansion. In FY25 growth decelerated sharply (+10% CER, +6% reported) and FY26 is guided to decline −5% to −13% at CER (mid-point −9%). The deceleration reflects: (1) intensifying competition from Eli Lilly's Mounjaro/Zepbound; (2) US pricing collapse under the Most Favored Nations (MFN) agreement signed November 2025; (3) US market share erosion — Novo's GLP-1 diabetes value share fell from 55.1% to 45.8% in a single year; (4) compound patent expiry of semaglutide in selected International Operations markets; (5) continued compounding by US pharmacies despite FDA grace period expiry May 2025. Obesity franchise growth (Wegovy +41% CER) remains strong but is being offset by declines elsewhere. The stock is down ~55% over 12 months and ~70% from its June 2024 peak of $137.

  • FY2022: DKK 66.7B — YoY +37% — Source: stockanalysis.com / NVO IR (Tier 1) Healthy Growth
  • FY2023: DKK 83.1B — YoY +25% — Source: stockanalysis.com / NVO IR (Tier 1) Healthy Growth
  • FY2024: DKK 73.8B — YoY −11% — Source: stockanalysis.com / NVO IR (Tier 1) BREACH (Driver: capex ramp from DKK 25.8B → 47.2B + Catalent-related CapEx; FCF NOT depressed by operating decline)
  • FY2025: DKK 59.0B — YoY −20% — Source: NVO Form 6-K (Tier 1) BREACH (Driver: capex ramp to DKK 60.1B + DKK 8B one-off restructuring charges + gross margin compression from Catalent integration)

Breach Assessment: TWO consecutive breach years (FY24 −11%, FY25 −20%). 5-year FCF CAGR (FY21 → FY25) = +5% (well below revenue 5-year CAGR of +22%). The FCF decline is driven by a structural capex cycle (DKK 6B in FY21 → DKK 60B in FY25, a 9.5x increase) to build out manufacturing capacity for GLP-1 demand, partially compounded by acquisition integration costs. Verdict: HIGH VOLATILITY High Volatility

FCF Growth Calibration: The 5-year FCF CAGR (+5%) is materially below revenue CAGR (+22%). This is the opposite of typical concern (FCF compounding faster than revenue); here, capex intensity is consuming the operating cash flow growth. Management FY26 guidance shows capex declining from DKK 60B to DKK 55B, with further reductions expected thereafter, implying FCF should recover toward normalised levels as the capex wave rolls off. Future DCF scenarios must reconcile two forces: (1) FY26 trough from pricing pressure + patent cliff, and (2) FCF margin recovery beyond FY26 as capex normalises.

Pre-Model Audit — Step B: FCF Base Normalisation

Most Recent Full-Year Reported FCF (FY25): DKK 59.0B (source: stockanalysis.com / NVO IR; using OCF DKK 119.1B less PP&E capex DKK 60.1B).

One-time items examined:

  • Company-wide transformation / restructuring charges: ~DKK 8B in FY25 — impacts operating profit by an estimated ~DKK 6-7B after tax, flowing through OCF. Source: NVO Form 6-K FY25 (Tier 1); explicitly quantified by management. Direction: Depresses FCF.
  • 340B Drug Pricing Program provision: ~USD 400M charge in FY25 which management will REVERSE in FY26 (USD 4.2B total reversal planned). In FY25 this DEPRESSED FCF; the FY26 reversal will INFLATE FY26 FCF by ~DKK 28B — but NVO guides this out of "adjusted" FY26 numbers. Direction: Depresses FY25 FCF.
  • Executive severance / management transition costs: New CEO Mike Doustdar took over from Lars Fruergaard Jørgensen in July 2025. Severance included in administrative costs (up +13%). Estimated ~DKK 0.3B. Direction: Depresses FCF.
  • Catalent integration costs: Run through cost of goods sold and SG&A; ongoing amortisation/depreciation now part of base cost structure (NOT a one-off adjustment).
  • Akero acquisition ($4.7B): Closed Dec 2025; flows through investing activities (intangible purchases DKK 30.0B line). Does NOT depress standard FCF (OCF − PP&E) definition. Correctly excluded from our adjustment.
  • CapEx (PP&E) vs 3-year average: FY25 capex DKK 60.1B. FY23 DKK 25.8B, FY24 DKK 47.2B. 3-year average ~DKK 44B. FY25 capex is 35% above 3-year average — capex is still ramping, NOT at steady state. Management guides FY26 capex DKK 55B (down) with further declines beyond. We treat this as cyclical rather than one-off; no adjustment to normalise capex (as capex ramp is a deliberate strategic investment, not an accounting quirk).

Conclusion: Restructuring charges of DKK 8B (net) are the primary identifiable one-off depressant. Adjustment = ~14% of reported FCF — exceeds 10% threshold. Normalisation applied — use normalised FCF as DCF anchor.

Reported FY25 FCF: DKK 59.0B | Normalised FY25 FCF: DKK 67.0B (adds back ~DKK 8B restructuring)

USD conversion @ 6.24 DKK/USD spot (29 Jan 2026 per NVO): Normalised FCF ≈ USD 10.7B | Reported FCF ≈ USD 9.5B

Critical caveat: FY26 guided FCF is DKK 35-45B (midpoint DKK 40B ≈ USD 6.4B), materially BELOW the normalised FY25 figure. Our DCF anchors on normalised FY25 and applies a conservative Y1 growth rate to reflect the FY26 trough. An alternative "FY26-anchored DCF" using the DKK 40B guidance as the base FCF yields intrinsic values 30-40% lower across all scenarios — flagged in sensitivity.

Pre-Model Audit — Step C: FCF / NOPAT Ratio Check

GAAP Operating Profit FY25: DKK 127.7B

Effective Tax Rate FY25: 21.5% (source: NVO Form 6-K, DKK 28.1B tax / DKK 130.5B pretax)

NOPAT = DKK 127.7B × (1 – 0.215) = DKK 127.7B × 0.785 = DKK 100.2B

FCF / NOPAT (normalised basis) = DKK 67.0B / DKK 100.2B = 67% Below 70% — Investigate

Explanation: FCF/NOPAT of 67% is below the "normal" 85-100% range, reflecting the heavy capex cycle: capex of DKK 60B vs D&A of only DKK 22B means NVO is investing 2.7x its depreciation in new capacity. This is a capital-intensive growth investment phase, not an accounting quality issue. Working capital tied up in inventory (DKK ~80B+) also absorbs cash as manufacturing scales. Beyond FY26-27, as capex/D&A ratio normalises toward 1.2-1.5x, FCF/NOPAT should recover to the 80%+ range more typical of large pharma. DCF anchor remains the normalised FCF of DKK 67B; the ratio confirms this is a cyclically-depressed figure, not a run-rate steady state.

④ Management Commentary

FY25 Annual Results Call — Management Tone & Key Quotes (3 February 2026)

Overall Tone: Measured and sober; notably more cautious than prior years. First annual results under new CEO Mike Doustdar (promoted July 2025, replacing Lars Fruergaard Jørgensen). Tone reflected: acknowledging a "challenging year," flagging FY26 as a pricing-pressure year, but positioning for a volume-led multi-year recovery. No euphoric language; no victory laps. Markedly different from 2021-2023 tone.

Mike Doustdar (President & CEO): Framed 2025 as a year of delivering 10% CER sales growth and reaching nearly 46 million patients, while acknowledging difficulty. Flagged FY26 pricing headwinds in an increasingly competitive market, but expressed confidence in volume growth over coming years, citing promising early Wegovy pill uptake. Highlighted upcoming FY26 catalysts: regulatory decisions for Mim8 (haemophilia) and CagriSema (obesity), plus phase 3 readouts for etavopivat and ziltivekimab.

Key FY26 Guidance: Adjusted sales growth −5% to −13% at CER (adjusted figures exclude the USD 4.2B 340B Drug Pricing Program reversal). Non-adjusted FY26 sales growth would be −1% CER at midpoint; non-adjusted operating profit growth +11% CER. Adjusted operating profit growth −5% to −13% CER. FY26 capex DKK 55B (down from 60B). FY26 FCF DKK 35-45B. Effective tax rate 21-23%.

Strategic Milestones Completed in FY25: Wegovy pill FDA approval (22 Dec 2025, first oral GLP-1 for obesity); CagriSema submission to US FDA for obesity; semaglutide 7.2 mg submitted in US and EU; IcoSema (Kyinsu) EU approval for type 2 diabetes; closing of Akero acquisition ($4.7B, phase 3 FGF21 for MASH); acquisition of three former Catalent manufacturing sites (closed late 2024, integrated during 2025).

MFN Pricing Agreement (Nov 2025): Novo (with Lilly) signed a "Most Favoured Nations" agreement with the US Administration. Expanded Medicare Part D access for obesity medicines from mid-2026 in exchange for significantly lower prices, including a NovoCare direct-to-patient Wegovy offer at $199/month (vs ~$1000 list). Management positioned this as volume-unlocking despite the price concession.

FY25 Wegovy US prescription data (as of 23 Jan 2026): ~230,000 weekly Wegovy prescriptions, of which ~70,000 via self-pay channel (NovoCare Pharmacy) and ~50,000 weekly prescriptions for newly-launched Wegovy pill. Self-pay channel now ~30% of total Wegovy scripts.

Analyst Reaction & Market Concerns — Post-FY25 Results

Initial Stock Move: NVO (ADR) fell −14.64% on 4 Feb 2026 to close at $50.30, with a further ~5% decline in premarket the following day. Consensus had expected in-line FY26 guidance; the magnitude of the FY26 sales decline guidance (midpoint −9% CER adjusted) was materially worse than analyst models. Consensus Target: ~$47-51 (stockanalysis.com avg $51.00; investing.com avg $47.01). Consensus rating: "Hold" (11 analysts on SA; 5 buy/0 sell on investing.com). This is a dramatic change from the Buy/Strong Buy ratings NVO carried through 2024. GuruFocus GF Value ~$143 (showing GF Value estimates lag current deteriorated fundamentals).

Top Analyst Concerns:

  • Structural pricing pressure under MFN: The $199/month direct-to-patient Wegovy offer represents an ~80% discount to the prior ~$1000/month list. Analysts question whether volume gains can fully offset price erosion. NVO now has <50% US GLP-1 value share vs >80% in 2023.
  • Lilly's structural lead with tirzepatide: SURMOUNT-5 head-to-head trial showed Zepbound (tirzepatide) delivered 20.2% weight loss vs 13.7% for Wegovy — a meaningful efficacy gap. Lilly's pipeline (orforglipron oral, retatrutide triple agonist) threatens further share loss. Per Morningstar, Lilly held ~57% US GLP-1 Rx share by Q3 FY25.
  • Semaglutide compound patent cliff in International Operations: Patents expire in selected IO markets from 2026 onward. Indian/Chinese generic and biosimilar players are scaling rapidly. Generic semaglutide has launched in some emerging markets already.
  • Alzheimer's evoke trial failure: Semaglutide evoke and evoke+ phase 3 trials in early-stage Alzheimer's disease failed to show significant slowing of disease progression (announced Nov 2025). This closed off a multi-billion dollar label expansion opportunity that analysts had baked into long-term forecasts.
  • Capex still climbing: DKK 60B capex in FY25, DKK 55B guided for FY26 — 19% and 17% of revenue respectively. Building capacity for volumes that may come with sharply lower per-unit economics. ROIC on incremental invested capital is under pressure.
  • Governance turbulence: Extraordinary General Meeting November 2025 to elect new Board members after shareholder pressure. CEO change July 2025. Sustained volatility in strategic direction has created execution risk.

⑤ Red Flags — Six-Point Lynch Audit

Red Flag 1 — Margins Compressing

Status: CONFIRMED — Margins are compressing across the entire P&L. Gross margin 84.7% → 81.0% (−3.7pt YoY) driven by Catalent amortisation and lower realised prices. GAAP operating margin 44.2% → 41.3% (−2.9pt YoY). FY26 guidance implies further compression as US pricing headwinds amplify under MFN. FY25 Q4 standalone operating margin was 40.1% vs 42.9% Q4 FY24 — showing deterioration throughout the year. Watch: FY26 Q1 results; if gross margin falls below 80%, the valuation thesis needs to be re-anchored.

Red Flag 2 — Volume / User Declining

Status: WATCH ⚠️ — Total patient count nearly flat: 45.2M → 45.6M (+1% YoY). Diabetes patient count actually DECLINED from 43.0M → 42.0M (−2%). Obesity care patient count grew 2.2M → 3.6M (+64%). Underlying volumes are mixed: Obesity care volume growth strong (global branded obesity market +104% volume growth), but Novo's share of that market declining. Novo's global GLP-1 volume share 54.6% vs 55.1% a year earlier — modest share erosion despite market growth. In International Operations GLP-1 diabetes value share collapsed from 67.6% to 45.9% as tirzepatide entered. Key Point: Volumes are still growing but margin of error is thin; a consecutive year of market share loss plus pricing decline would put overall volumes at risk.

Red Flag 3 — FCF Volatile

Status: CONFIRMED — Two consecutive breach years. FCF (standard def.) DKK 83.1B (FY23) → 73.8B (FY24, −11%) → 59.0B (FY25, −20%). FY26 guided FCF DKK 35-45B, which at midpoint DKK 40B implies a further −32% decline. From peak FY23 to guided FY26 mid, FCF would have declined by more than 50% over 3 years. The drivers are primarily capex-related (strategic capacity buildout) plus restructuring and pricing pressure — not accounting manipulation — but the volatility is undeniable. FCF trajectory does not stabilise until FY27-28 per management commentary. HIGH VOLATILITY verdict from Step A reconfirmed.

Red Flag 4 — Capex >70% of Operating Cash Flow

Status: CONFIRMED — FY25 Capex (PP&E) DKK 60.1B / OCF DKK 119.1B = 50.5% capex/OCF ratio. Not at 70% but approaching worryingly high levels. Adding intangible asset purchases (DKK 30B, mostly Akero) brings total investing outflow to DKK 90B, 75% of OCF. The capex intensity reflects deliberate capacity buildout for GLP-1 volume growth, but the magnitude and velocity create risk: if volumes don't materialise at forecast levels (or materialise at sharply lower realised prices), capacity investments become stranded. Management guides capex to decline in coming years (FY26: DKK 55B), but peers like LLY are also in heavy capex mode — if both overbuild, the industry faces oversupply. Watch: Capex/OCF ratio should trend back below 35% by FY28 to confirm disciplined capital allocation.

Red Flag 5 — Debt Growing in Downturn

Status: CONFIRMED — Net debt has exploded over 24 months. FY22: net cash ~DKK 18B. FY23: net cash to moderate net debt ~DKK 30B. FY24: net debt grew dramatically post-Catalent to ~DKK 100B. FY25: net debt ~DKK 105B (USD ~16B), with gross debt at DKK 139B (doubled YoY) funding Catalent and Akero acquisitions. Debt issuance of DKK 103.9B in FY25 exceeded debt repayments of DKK 79.2B — net addition DKK 24.7B. Debt/Equity has risen to 67%. This is a company that historically carried virtually zero net debt now funding operations partly from debt, precisely as underlying FCF is deteriorating. Moody's and S&P still rate NVO AA-/Aa3 given FCF coverage, but the trajectory is adverse. Watch: If FY26 FCF lands at bottom of DKK 35B guidance and M&A continues, debt/EBITDA could exceed 1.5x.

Red Flag 6 — Management Using Uncertainty / Headwinds Language

Status: CONFIRMED — Management commentary is heavily laden with hedging language. The word "headwinds" appears throughout the FY25 announcement. CEO Doustdar: "2025 being a challenging year for the company" and explicit forward-looking statement: "In 2026, Novo Nordisk will face pricing headwinds in an increasingly competitive market." Financial communications also introduced a NEW non-IFRS "adjusted" metric for FY26 that excludes the 340B reversal — non-IFRS metrics introduced mid-crisis to frame numbers more favourably are a classic warning sign. The lowered growth expectations in July 2025 mid-year (sales growth from 13-21% down to 8-14%) and again in Q3 2025 (down to 8-11%) show the guidance trajectory has been systematically missed on the downside. Tone of humility is appropriate to the situation, but Lynch would score this as the textbook "we're facing temporary headwinds" narrative that often presages further downgrades.

⑥ Invert — Charlie Munger Style

Why This Could Be a Bad Long-Term Investment

The bull case for NVO rests on a premise that may no longer hold: that Novo Nordisk remains a structurally dominant GLP-1 franchise with durable pricing power. The evidence from 2024-2025 suggests this premise is cracking. Lilly's tirzepatide has better clinical efficacy (20.2% vs 13.7% weight loss), better commercial momentum, a deeper next-generation pipeline (orforglipron oral, retatrutide triple agonist at 24% weight loss in phase 2), and substantially higher invested capital capacity being brought online. Meanwhile, compounding pressures on Novo's franchise — MFN pricing, compound patent expiry in IO markets, Indian/Chinese biosimilar players, Medicare Part D inclusion at negotiated prices — all point to structurally lower gross margin per patient going forward. The company just took on $16B+ in debt to acquire Catalent manufacturing capacity that was justified by a demand forecast made in 2023 when Novo had 80%+ market share and list pricing; by 2026 that demand forecast is being delivered at 50% of assumed prices with 50% of assumed market share. The pivot to Wegovy pill and CagriSema is strategically correct but Lilly is competing in both categories with products that have pharmacological and commercial advantages. In a bear case, Novo faces 3-5 years of declining revenue per patient, margin compression, and elevated capex — during which dividend growth stalls and the stock remains dead money.

What Could Invalidate the Bull Thesis Despite Good Financials

Even if Novo's headline FY26 revenue comes in at the top of guidance (−5% CER) and the Wegovy pill achieves commercial success, the bull thesis fails if: (1) Wegovy pill cannibalises injectable Wegovy at lower prices — converting a $1,000/month injectable revenue stream into a $300/month oral revenue stream is negative mix shift even if adoption is strong. (2) CagriSema approval in Q4 2026 launches into a market where Lilly's retatrutide (potentially 25-30% weight loss) is already pivoting. CagriSema delivered 14.2% weight loss in REIMAGINE 2, which is less than single-agent Wegovy at higher doses (20.7% for semaglutide 7.2mg). The "second-generation" positioning may not hold. (3) US Medicare Part D inclusion brings mandatory price negotiation into obesity drugs by 2028. What starts as a volume tailwind through Medicare access could become a price-negotiation headwind when Ozempic/Wegovy become negotiation-eligible under the IRA. (4) International Operations become a price commodity market faster than expected — Chinese and Indian biosimilar production scaling. (5) The Foundation's 77% voting control becomes a governance negative rather than positive if it prevents strategic asset sales or restructuring that minority shareholders would prefer.

Assumptions That Must Go Right for This to Succeed

The DCF Base scenario at ~$39-48 (r=8-9%) requires: (1) FY26 is the trough year. Consensus FY26 EPS (USD) around $3.00 should be the bottom, with FY27 resuming growth. (2) Wegovy pill launch scales without cannibalising injectable more than ~20%. The 50,000 weekly prescriptions in the first 3 weeks is encouraging but must scale to 250,000+ without collapsing injectable volume. (3) CagriSema gains meaningful market share in the 20%+ weight loss tier despite Lilly competitive pressure. (4) Capex peaks in FY26 at DKK 55B and declines meaningfully thereafter — enabling FCF recovery toward DKK 80-100B by FY28-29. (5) The Novo Nordisk Foundation does not force value-destroying strategic choices (R&D overinvestment, lottery-ticket M&A). (6) Int'l Operations volume growth of 70%+ in obesity continues for 2-3 more years, offsetting US pricing and diabetes share loss. (7) No major new entrants succeed in next 36 months — Pfizer oral GLP-1, Amgen MariTide, Structure Therapeutics, Viking Therapeutics all must stumble. All seven items are independent risks; probability-weighted outcome skews Base-Low.

Structural Weaknesses — The Munger Pre-Mortem

1. Concentrated product risk: Semaglutide (Ozempic + Wegovy + Rybelsus) represents DKK 228B of DKK 309B total sales — 74% of revenue from a single molecule franchise. Even with patent extensions through 2031 in the US, this is extreme concentration by any large pharma standard. 2. Governance structure: Novo Nordisk Foundation controls A-shares with 77% voting power vs ~27% economic interest. This preserves long-term orientation but also means minority shareholder-friendly actions (large buybacks, spin-offs, strategic refocusing) face a gatekeeper with different incentives. The November 2025 EGM to replace Board members suggests this tension is intensifying. 3. Geographic concentration: 56% of sales in US Operations which is now the pricing-pressure epicentre. International Operations growth (+14% CER) is the offsetting engine but comes with compound patent expiry risks. 4. Capex overhang: DKK 260B of combined CapEx + M&A over the past 3 years (Catalent $16.5B, Akero $4.7B, capacity expansion DKK 100B+). If demand disappoints, write-downs become inevitable. Catalent assets alone could face impairment if US manufacturing capacity utilisation falls below plan. 5. DKK exposure: Reporting currency DKK pegged to EUR; most costs and some sales in DKK/EUR, but 40%+ of sales in USD. When USD weakens against DKK (as it has since 2024), reported results look worse than constant-currency reality. Hedging programs only partially offset. 6. New CEO, new strategy risk: Mike Doustdar inherits a company in crisis mode. His first Capital Markets Day (scheduled 21 September 2026) will set 2026-2030 strategic aspirations. This creates strategic uncertainty — a complete strategic reset is possible — and the market will be unforgiving of any plan that signals slower growth or heavier investment.

⑦ Golden Tips — Lynch Analyst Watchlist

Golden Tip 1 — GAAP vs Non-GAAP: Novo's Clean Accounting (A Positive)

Unlike US peers where non-GAAP vs GAAP gaps of 10-20 percentage points are common due to SBC, amortisation and one-off "adjusted" items, Novo Nordisk's accounting is remarkably clean. FY25 adjusted EPS DKK 26.17 vs GAAP diluted EPS DKK 23.03 — a ~13% gap, primarily driven by the one-off restructuring charge of DKK 8B and impairment/amortisation adjustments. The Novo Nordisk Foundation ownership structure produces minimal SBC (~1% of revenue vs US peers 3-8%), avoiding the "EPS inflation via buybacks that offset SBC dilution" trap common in US pharma. However, management's introduction of NEW non-IFRS metrics for FY26 (excluding the 340B reversal of USD 4.2B) is a fresh warning sign — Lynch would flag it and track reconciliation vigorously. Report both: FY26 reported sales growth −1% CER vs adjusted −9% CER. The two numbers tell different stories and analysts will anchor on whichever management emphasises. Always demand the reported (IFRS) number first.

Golden Tip 2 — FCF vs Net Income: The Capex Cycle Matters Here

FY25 GAAP Net Income DKK 102.4B vs standard-definition FCF DKK 59.0B — FCF is 42% LOWER than net income. This is the INVERSE of the SaaS/subscription pattern where FCF typically exceeds net income (thanks to deferred revenue + SBC addback). Here, FCF is depressed by two things: (1) capex at DKK 60B vs depreciation at DKK 22B (2.7x D&A), and (2) working capital absorption tied up in inventory for global rollout. For Novo at this moment, Net Income is the cleaner economic earnings measure because the capex is transitory (a capacity buildout cycle) and the D&A will rise to meet the capex base over 3-5 years. An analyst using FCF as the valuation anchor today will underestimate the company's normalised earning power; one using Net Income will be closer to run-rate reality. This is a rare case where FCF and NI disagree materially and both are "right" depending on time horizon: short-term (2-3 years) FCF matters for dividend sustainability; long-term (5+ years) Net Income matters for intrinsic value because capex normalises.

Golden Tip 3 — Revenue Up, Margins Down: The Classic Lynch Warning Signal

Novo Nordisk is delivering the textbook "revenue up, margins down" pattern Peter Lynch flagged as a red flag in One Up On Wall Street. FY25: Revenue +10% CER, GAAP operating margin −2.9pt. FY26 guidance: Adjusted sales −5 to −13%, adjusted op profit −5 to −13% — the fact that operating profit decline matches sales decline indicates operating leverage is already fully priced in; there's no margin cushion left. This is the pattern of a business losing pricing power, typically 6-18 months before the market fully prices the deterioration. The stock has now priced a lot of this (down 55% in 12 months) but the margin trajectory through FY27-28 will determine whether $40/share represents value or a value trap. Inflection indicator to watch: The quarter in which gross margin stops declining and stabilises. When you see that — likely FY26 Q4 or FY27 Q1 — the stock likely bottoms and re-rates. Until then, the margin-compression narrative dominates. Lynch would say: "Watch for the margin bottom; buy when it happens, not before."

Golden Tip 4 — Capex Pattern: From Asset-Light to Asset-Heavy

Novo Nordisk has transformed from an asset-light, FCF-gusher pharma company into an asset-heavy capacity builder. 2021 capex: DKK 6.3B (4.5% of revenue). 2025 capex: DKK 60.1B (19.5% of revenue). That's a 10x increase in absolute capex and a 4.3x increase in capex intensity. For comparison, asset-heavy semiconductors run at 25-35% capex/revenue; asset-light pharma traditionally runs at 5-10%. Novo has crossed into semiconductor-like capital intensity, and this permanently changes the valuation framework. Historically NVO traded at 35x P/E because it was a compounder that didn't need reinvestment; now at 12x P/E because it needs heavy reinvestment with uncertain incremental returns. Management has guided capex to decline after 2026, but history shows capex cycles typically run 1-2 years longer than guided. If capex normalises back to 8-10% of revenue by 2028 (DKK 25-30B annually), FCF margins recover to ~30% and the company can re-rate. If capex stays elevated (LLY-like, still climbing), the multiple compression is permanent. Any single-year capex guidance increase beyond DKK 55B for FY26, or the absence of a meaningful step-down signalled for FY27, should be treated as a Sell trigger. Any confirmed step-down sequence DKK 55B → 45B → 35B over 2026-2028 would be a re-rating catalyst.

⑧ Additional Deep-Dive: GLP-1 Market 5-10 Year Projection

Market Size Projections — Consensus View (2030-2035)

The consensus range for global GLP-1 market size by 2030-2035 is extraordinarily wide, reflecting deep uncertainty:

  • Goldman Sachs (anti-obesity drugs only): ~$100B by 2030 (with ~15M US adults treated, 13% penetration of eligible population)
  • Evaluate Pharma (2025 World Preview): 5 major GLP-1 products (Ozempic, Wegovy, Rybelsus, Mounjaro, Zepbound) to generate ~$470B cumulative revenue through 2030. In 2030, Mounjaro $36B (world's top-selling drug), Ozempic $24B, Zepbound $25B, Wegovy $18B, CagriSema $15B.
  • Morningstar: Diabetes + obesity GLP-1 market reaches $180B by 2034 (ahead of consensus)
  • Grand View Research: $66B (2025) → $185B (2033), 12.4% CAGR
  • Towards Healthcare: $58B (2026) → $133B (2035), 9.6% CAGR
  • Novata Market Research: $53B (2024) → $157B (2030), 17.5% CAGR
  • Future Market Insights (obesity-only subset): $8B (2025) → $65B (2035), 23.1% CAGR

Base-case synthesis for decision-making purposes: Total GLP-1 market (diabetes + obesity combined, at manufacturer pricing) expands from ~$65B in 2025 to ~$150-180B by 2030, then ~$180-220B by 2035. The range narrows after 2030 as pricing normalises and biosimilars enter. Obesity-care subset grows faster than diabetes subset.

Novo + Lilly Duopoly Erosion — 5 to 10 Year Outlook

Current market share (Q3 FY25, US Rx basis per Morningstar): Eli Lilly 57%, Novo Nordisk 43%. Globally (volume), Novo retains leadership at 54.6% GLP-1 volume share across diabetes and obesity combined (Q4 FY25, IQVIA).

How much of total GLP-1 growth to 2035 can Novo capture? Analytical framework:

  • Base case (40-45% share by 2030, declining to 30-35% by 2035): Novo captures ~$55-70B of GLP-1 revenue in 2030, ~$60-75B by 2035. Supports Novo Nordisk total revenue of ~$75B by 2030 (close to Evaluate Pharma's $84B estimate) with some offset from semaglutide IO LOE and diabetes share erosion.
  • Bull case (50%+ share retention): Wegovy pill + CagriSema + next-gen amycretin and zenagamtide extend Novo's innovation track into 2028-2030. Requires Novo to match or exceed Lilly's pipeline pace. Captures ~$75-90B by 2030.
  • Bear case (30% share by 2030, 20% by 2035): Lilly tirzepatide gap widens, orforglipron oral wins the oral category, retatrutide triple agonist captures the 25%+ weight loss tier, and Indian/Chinese biosimilars commoditise semaglutide in emerging markets. Captures only ~$40-50B by 2030.

How much goes to Lilly? Evaluate Pharma projects Lilly will be the world's #1 Rx seller at $113B by 2030, with tirzepatide products alone at ~$61B. Lilly's share in the GLP-1 subset likely stays 50-55% through 2030 barring a major clinical setback, making Lilly the primary beneficiary of market growth.

How much goes to "other" entrants? Expected 10-20% combined share by 2030, rising to 25-35% by 2035:

  • Pfizer: Oral GLP-1 pipeline (danuglipron discontinued 2023 after liver tox; second-gen molecules in development). Likely 2-4% share by 2030 if development progresses.
  • Amgen (MariTide/AMG133): Monthly dosing GLP-1/GIPR antagonist; ~20% weight loss in phase 2. Phase 3 underway. Potential 4-8% share if convenience advantage translates.
  • Structure Therapeutics: Oral small-molecule GLP-1 agonists in development.
  • Viking Therapeutics: Dual GLP-1/GIP agonist VK2735 in phase 3.
  • Roche: Acquired Carmot Therapeutics 2023 for $2.7B; pipeline includes injectable + oral GLP-1 candidates.
  • Merck (via Hansoh license, HS-10535): Licensed GLP-1 receptor agonist for cardiometabolic diseases; early development.
  • Indian/Chinese biosimilar players: Glenmark (Lirafit in India), Biocon, Aurobindo. Expected to capture 30-50% share in emerging markets (not US/EU) post-patent expiry, at 1/5th to 1/10th of branded prices. Significant driver of Novo's IO pricing pressure.
  • BMY, Merck & Co: Limited direct exposure to GLP-1 currently; BMY has no announced competitive molecule; Merck's Hansoh licensed molecule is early-stage.

Wider Consumer & Food Trends Benefiting GLP-1 Uptake

GLP-1 adoption is reinforced by and reinforces several macro consumer trends:

  • Obesity prevalence still rising globally: World Obesity Atlas 2023 forecasts >50% of global population will be overweight or obese by 2035 (vs 38% in 2020). CDC estimates US obesity-related medical costs at $173B annually. This expands the addressable patient population mechanically.
  • Mainstream cultural normalisation of GLP-1s: 23% of US households already have a GLP-1 user per Circana Nov 2025 research. Morgan Stanley estimates 12.4% of US adults currently using GLP-1s (up from 5.8% in Feb 2024, per Gallup National Health Index). Celebrity endorsement, direct-to-patient telehealth channels (Hims & Hers, NovoCare, Ro), and ~45% projected penetration of the obese/overweight eligible population by 2030 drive continued sign-ups.
  • Expanding clinical indications: Wegovy approved for cardiovascular risk reduction (SELECT trial 2024), MASH (FDA approved Dec 2025), sleep apnea, kidney disease (FLOW trial), and now being tested in Alzheimer's (failed 2025), addiction, osteoarthritis, and certain cancers. Each indication expansion enlarges the addressable population and increases insurance-reimbursement justification.
  • Payer coverage expansion: US Medicare Part D coverage for obesity medicines begins mid-2026 (~5M additional covered lives). Commercial payer coverage approaching 50M lives covered. International Operations expansion continues with launches in >50 countries for Wegovy.
  • Food & beverage industry shifts (both tailwind and structural shift): Circana projects GLP-1 user households will represent 35% of US food & beverage units sold by 2030 (up from 23% today). GLP-1 users show:
    • Negative for snacks/sweets/alcohol: Savoury snacks −10%, sweets −10%, baked foods and cookies similar declines. Alcohol consumption cut 25% (Morgan Stanley research).
    • Positive for protein, functional foods, produce: Yogurt +17%, fresh fruit +14%, fresh meat +15%, protein bars/shakes rising. Nestlé launched "Vital Pursuit" GLP-1-tailored food line 2024.
    • Restaurant spending up (GLP-1 users outsource meal preparation more frequently as household appetites diverge).
    • Total calorie reduction across the US population estimated at 3-5% by 2030 per Barclays/Morgan Stanley research.
  • Protein/satiety/functional food boom: Companies benefiting (tailwinds): Danone (Oikos high-protein yogurt), Premier Protein, Orgain, Gatorade/Liquid I.V. (hydration), Vital Pursuit (Nestlé). Companies facing headwinds (structural): PepsiCo Frito-Lay (−2% organic North American foods growth Q1 FY25); Mondelēz (−4.1% NA revenue decline); Campbell's (lowered 2025 forecast on cookies); JM Smucker (Hostess acquisition underperforming).
  • Alcohol decline: Nearly 25% of GLP-1 users stopped drinking alcohol completely (Morgan Stanley). This compounds pre-existing "sober-curious" Gen Z trends. Implications for Diageo, AB InBev, Constellation Brands, and wine/spirits more broadly.
  • Weight-management programs pivoting to GLP-1 integration: WeightWatchers partnered with telehealth GLP-1 prescribers; Noom integrated GLP-1 support; virtually all diet-culture brands now repositioning around medication-assisted weight loss rather than diet + exercise alone.

Implications for Novo investment thesis: The macro tailwind for GLP-1 adoption is overwhelmingly positive for the category. The critical question for Novo shareholders is whether Novo can retain a meaningful share of a growing pie — a 35% share of a $150B market (~$52B NVO GLP-1 revenue) is better than 55% share of a $65B market (~$36B NVO GLP-1 revenue today). Market growth plus some share loss can still produce absolute revenue growth. This is why management's volume-focused messaging ("we remain confident in our ability to drive volume growth over the coming years") is not wishful thinking — it is plausible if share losses stabilise.
